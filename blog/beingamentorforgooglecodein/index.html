Being A Mentor For Google Code-in

March 4, 2020    experiences

About Google Code-In

Google Code-In is an one of a kind online contest where pre-university students (ages 13 to 17) are invited to take part and experience open-source development. They have a wide variety of byte-sized tasks from various participating organisations, easy for beginners to jump in and get started no matter what skills they have.

What Do Mentors Do

Mentors are the primary helping hands for the organisations. Mentors create tasks and look over their proper completion by the participants. Mentors are also always ready to help the participants whenever they need any help or guidance. Mentors might also be responsible for judging submissions and granting task completion status for some of the organisation (w.r.t my experience).

Here’s the formal set of responsibilities, as mentioned by Google.

How To Apply

I found out that Tensorflow was taking in this year’s Google Code-in 2020, this was the tenth edition. So I connected with a product manager via Twitter and she readily made me a mentor for Tensorflow. Here’s a descriptive video, directly from Google Open Source, you must have a look at this.

Tasks

Tasks are mainly ranging in the fields of:

  • Coding
  • Documentation & Training
  • Outreach & Research
  • Quality Assurance
  • Design
  • Miscellaneous

The difficulty of tasks depend from org to org, it is always better to mentor for a known organization whose technology is well-known to you as it would help you clear the doubts and concepts of participants.

As a mentor you would also have to create tasks which will be approved by the org admins before the starting of the contest. Two of my tasks were accepted and I felt really happy when I saw the participants working on it. :)

Rewards & Prizes

Don’t worry about that. It’s Google man. :)

Just for the account they have digital certificates, T-shirts, Jackets and at the best a Grand Prize Trip (about which I have no idea :P).

Thoughts & Suggestions

I personally felt it as a great way to encourage young kids into technology especially open source development. Also, I felt that, at the age of 16-17 I knew nothing about open source development. It is so inspiring to be able to help out these bright young minds and observe how they think and approach problems. Although the entire experience is depending a lot upon the tasks assigned and the mentors assigned by an organisation, it is a great experience in total.
